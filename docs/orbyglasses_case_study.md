## The Technology: A Multi-Modal AI Solution

OrbyGlasses addresses these challenges through an innovative integration of multiple AI technologies working in harmony. At its core, the system represents a bio-mimetic approach to navigation assistance, modeling itself after the natural echolocation abilities of bats and other animals. This biological inspiration is not merely metaphorical but forms the foundation of a comprehensive approach that combines computer vision, depth estimation, spatial audio processing, and natural language understanding.

The computer vision component utilizes YOLOv12n, a state-of-the-art object detection model specifically optimized for real-time performance on Apple Silicon devices. This choice of model reflects a careful balance between accuracy and efficiency, as the system must process frames continuously while maintaining high frame rates. The YOLO model is trained on the COCO dataset and customized with priority classes specifically relevant to navigation—people, vehicles, obstacles, and environmental features that are most critical for safe movement.

Complementing this visual processing is an advanced depth estimation system that leverages Depth Anything V2, a transformer-based model from Hugging Face. This model creates real-time depth maps that allow the system to understand not just what objects are present in the environment, but where they are in three-dimensional space. The integration of depth information is crucial for the system's ability to distinguish between distant objects that pose no immediate threat and nearby obstacles that require immediate action or navigation adjustments.

The audio processing system represents one of the most innovative aspects of OrbyGlasses. Rather than simply providing verbal descriptions, the system generates spatial audio cues that simulate bat echolocation. This bio-mimetic approach uses frequency, volume, and stereo positioning to convey spatial information. Closer objects are represented by higher frequencies and louder volumes, while the stereo positioning indicates the horizontal location relative to the user. This creates an intuitive audio landscape that experienced users can interpret almost as naturally as visual information.

## User Demographics and Market Analysis

The target demographic for OrbyGlasses encompasses a diverse global population with varying needs, backgrounds, and technological comfort levels. Understanding this diversity is crucial for appreciating both the challenges and opportunities within this market segment. The 285 million people with vision impairment represent a complex demographic with different age distributions, causes of vision loss, and adaptive needs.

The congenitally blind community, those born without sight, represents a significant and unique user group. Globally, approximately 285,000 people in the United States alone were born without sight, though the worldwide figure is significantly larger. These individuals have never experienced visual input and have developed alternative navigation systems based on spatial memory, tactile feedback, and auditory cues. For this group, OrbyGlasses offers the opportunity to enhance their existing navigation strategies with additional real-time environmental awareness, potentially reducing the cognitive load of maintaining constant situational awareness.

In contrast, the acquired blindness community—those who lose sight later in life—represents the fastest-growing segment within the vision-impaired population. This group faces unique challenges as they must adapt to a world of movement and navigation that previously relied heavily on visual input. The average age of this group is increasing as age-related eye diseases become more prevalent, and the leading causes include diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, and age-related macular degeneration. These individuals often have more experience with technology and may be more open to adopting new assistive technologies, though they may also carry more preconceptions about what is possible with assistive technology.

The low vision community, comprising 246 million people worldwide, represents the largest and most diverse segment of the target market. These individuals retain some sight but have sufficient visual impairment to require assistive technology for safe navigation. This group includes individuals with peripheral vision loss, central vision loss, and varying degrees of clarity. For them, OrbyGlasses can serve as an augmentation technology, enhancing their remaining vision with audio and spatial cues. This segment is particularly important because it includes many individuals who are still active in the workforce and educational systems, creating strong demand for technologies that enable continued independence.

Geographically, the market presents interesting variations. In developed markets such as North America, Western Europe, and East Asia, there is strong technological adoption and robust healthcare infrastructure to support the distribution and support of assistive technologies. These markets also have established disability rights frameworks and social support systems that can facilitate adoption. However, the fastest growth is occurring in emerging markets across Asia-Pacific, Latin America, and other regions where aging populations and increasing diabetes rates are driving up the number of people with vision impairment.

## Competitive Landscape and Market Positioning

The assistive technology market for navigation presents both opportunities and challenges for OrbyGlasses. Current solutions range from traditional tools like white canes and guide dogs to emerging technologies like smartphone apps and specialized smart glasses. Each solution has established user bases and specific advantages, creating a complex competitive landscape that OrbyGlasses navigates through its unique multi-modal approach and privacy-first philosophy.

Traditional solutions remain dominant in many markets. White canes, used by approximately 80% of blind individuals, provide excellent ground-level obstacle detection and surface texture information. However, they offer limited range and cannot detect overhead obstacles or provide spatial awareness of complex environments. Guide dogs, while providing excellent mobility assistance, require significant training time, have limited lifespans of 8-12 years, and cost $50,000+ for training and care over their lifetime. These traditional solutions also require significant infrastructure, such as guide dog training schools and ongoing veterinary care.

Smartphone applications like BlindSquare and Seeing AI have gained significant adoption, with approximately 60% of tech-savvy blind users utilizing some form of mobile navigation assistance. These applications provide valuable features including GPS navigation, object recognition, and text reading. However, they have significant limitations including the need to handle a phone while navigating, limited range of detection, and potential distractions from other phone functions. Many users report that handling a phone while walking creates safety risks and reduces confidence in navigation.

Existing smart glasses solutions, such as OrCam and Aira, represent the closest competitors to OrbyGlasses. These systems offer hands-free operation and advanced computer vision capabilities. However, they suffer from several limitations that OrbyGlasses addresses. Many existing solutions require cloud connectivity, creating privacy concerns and reliability issues in areas with poor connectivity. They also tend to be expensive ($2,000-4,000), limiting accessibility, and often focus on specific tasks like text reading rather than comprehensive navigation assistance.

OrbyGlasses differentiates itself through several key advantages. The privacy-first approach with 100% local processing addresses major concerns about data privacy and security that many users have with cloud-dependent solutions. The multi-modal feedback system combining spatial audio, verbal guidance, and bio-mimetic cues provides more comprehensive environmental awareness than single-modality solutions. The integration of social navigation features, with region-specific cultural awareness, addresses the complex social navigation challenges that occur in crowded public spaces.

## Technical Architecture and Innovation

The technical architecture of OrbyGlasses demonstrates sophisticated engineering to balance multiple competing requirements: real-time performance, battery life, accuracy, and user experience. The system architecture follows a modular, pipeline-based approach that allows each component to operate efficiently while maintaining cohesion with the overall system.

The core processing pipeline begins with camera input, typically captured at 320x320 resolution to balance visual detail with processing efficiency. The choice of 20 FPS as a target reflects careful optimization between the need for real-time feedback and the computational constraints of portable devices. Each frame undergoes object detection using the YOLOv12n model, which has been specifically optimized for this application through careful selection of confidence thresholds and prioritization of navigation-relevant object classes.

The depth estimation component represents a particular technical challenge, as stereo depth estimation is computationally intensive and real-time depth estimation on a single camera is complex. The system uses Depth Anything V2, a transformer-based model that provides excellent depth accuracy while remaining computationally feasible for real-time operation. To maintain frame rates, the system implements a frame-skipping approach, calculating depth only every fourth frame and interpolating for intermediate frames. This optimization maintains performance while ensuring that depth information remains current enough for safe navigation.

The audio processing system implements several innovative approaches to spatial audio generation. The frequency mapping algorithm converts distance information to audio frequency, with closer objects represented by higher frequencies. The volume mapping uses an inverse square law approximation to make closer objects louder, while stereo positioning indicates horizontal location relative to the user. This creates an intuitive audio landscape that experienced users can interpret almost as naturally as visual information.

The integration of large language models for narrative generation adds another layer of complexity. The system uses Gemma3:4b for general navigation guidance and Moondream for visual scene interpretation. The challenge lies in balancing the rich information provided by LLMs with the real-time requirements of navigation. The system addresses this through careful interval management, generating detailed narratives only when appropriate and relying on simpler audio cues for immediate guidance needs.
